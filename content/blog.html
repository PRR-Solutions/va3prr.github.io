2023-12-10 - SSTV Crash Course

The question I've heard most frequently lately is what do I need to get started with SSTV?
These days, if you've got a computer or smartphone and a radio, you've probably already got enough to play with SSTV. If you've got proper audio cables to connect your radio directly to the computer or smartphone, you'll have an even better experience, but my tests show that you can generally get on just fine without it.

Acoustic Coupling
If you don't have the audio connector cable for your radio, you can use acoustic coupling - this just means holding your radio close enough to your phone/computer for each device to be able to hear the other. Whether you're connecting directly to the radio or using acoustic coupling, it's good practice to fine tune the input and output volume for each device; if your audio is too low, the receiving station may not be able to decode you, and if it's too high you'll see distortions in the image.
If you're going the acoustic coupling route, it's important to keep in mind that background noise will interfere with encoding and decoding images. You'll get the best results in a quiet environment, but SSTV appears to be surprisingly tolerant of ambient noise - all of SSTV's tones fit in the 1200-2300 Hz range, so wind and road noise don't seem to disturb image encoding/decoding much, but voices, beeps and whistles will cross this frequency range and cause noticeable interference.
Encoding/Decoding
Initially, this was job for hardware, but encoding and decoding images is largely done through software now (unless you're lucky enough to have an SSTV converter). There is a range of free and paid software available for Windows, Linux, Mac, Android, and iOS. I've had at least some experience with most of the following apps, but I mostly use QSSTV on Linux.
Android
  • Robot 36 (Free decoder)
  • SSTV Encoder (Free encoder)
  • DroidSSTV (encoder/decoder, non-free)

iOS
  • SSTV Slow Scan TV (encoder/decoder, non-free)

Windows
  • MMSSTV (encoder/decoder, free)
  • RX-SSTV (decoder, free)

Linux
  • QSSTV (encoder/decoder, free and open-source)

Mac
  • MultiMode (encoder/decoder, non-free)
  • KD6CJI MultiScan 3B (encoder/decoder, appears free)

The fun part!

Generally, the software tends to be self-explanatory, but here are some important tips:
  • Many applications will allow you to stop and start the decoder while you're not receiving. In some cases, the decoder is on by default, and off by default in others. Verify that your decoder is running if you plan to receive an image!
  • Check your Auto-Slant setting - synchronization errors can cause received images to appear slanted, turn auto-slant on to correct this
  • Make sure you're using the correct mode! Most receiving software can automatically detect the mode being used (if it decodes the VIS code mentioned in my previous post), but nothing prevents you from transmitting PD240 when you were hoping for Robot 36 (4 minutes to transmit an image, rather than 36 seconds).
I'll talk about modes and my rationale for selecting Robot 36 for the net in a later post, but now it's time for a(nother) cup of coffee.



2023-12-08 - New stuff to learn!

This was a fun net, we had a total of 4 check-ins, and I think 6 images sent - check out the gallery!

From what I've heard tonight, and from some of the images I've received, it sounds like a few of us had issues with the software properly detecting the start and stop of an image. A couple of images submitted to the gallery were cropped from screenshots in the Robot36 android application.

If I understand correctly, all modern SSTV encoders begin with a calibration header and a VIS code before transmitting actual image content. I suspect that the images that failed to decode properly were a result of missing part or all of these packets. The calibration header serves as both a tuning and a timing reference; failing to properly decode this header can result in blurring, misalignment, colour smearing, and other artefacts. Following the calibration header, the VIS (Vertical Interval Signalling) Code tells the receiver which mode will be used to transmit the image. I'm not sure if failure to decode the VIS code will cause failure to decode an image in all cases, but it is a part of the initial signalling process.

Since these packets both happen at the very beginning of the transmission, I think it's possible there's a combination of three scenarios causing our failures to decode. Scenario 1 is just the nature of simplex; sometimes you miss stuff. Scenario 2 has the PTT being triggered late, missing the header and VIS code on transmit. Scenario 3 has the operator hearing the SSTV tones and triggering the receiver late.


In the case of the image I sent (shown above), you can see how the loss of sync causes misalignment of colours; the background of this image is originally white, but you can see vertical blocks of white, yellow, and blue in the received version. Explaining why this happens is a little further ahead than I've been able to learn so far, but my understanding indicates it's an issue with loss of sync. Definitely a topic for discussion later on down the road.
